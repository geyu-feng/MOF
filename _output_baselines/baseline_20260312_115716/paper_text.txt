ABSTRACT  
Hypothesis: Metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) have been widely studied for Cr(VI) adsorption in water. Theo -
retically, numerous MOFs can be synthesised by assembling diverse metals and ligands. However, the traditional

1. Introduction 
Environmental pollution problems caused by the high toxicity of 
heavy metal ions in aqueous environments have drawn widespread 
attention [1,2]. Cr(VI) has strong carcinogenic properties and intense 
toxicity; it is pervasive in the environment and easily migrates and ac -
cumulates in living organisms. Therefore, effective removal technolo -
gies are highly sought after [3,4]. Currently, there are several 
approaches for the removal of Cr(VI) from aqueous media, including ion 
exchange, biological treatment, chemical reduction, precipitation, 
electrolysis and adsorption [5]. Adsorption is an effective, simple, and 
environmentally friendly method. Activated carbon, chitosan, and other 
resins are widely used for this purpose. However, activated carbon has 
drawbacks such as poor reproducibility, high processing costs, and 
limited application range [6]. Chitosan, on the other hand, is unstable 
and easily dissolves in acidic water [7,8]. Thus, the development of 
highly efficient adsorbents is particularly important in adsorption 
technology. 
Metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) are periodic reticulated structures 
composed of coordination metals and organic ligands that have poten -
tial as adsorbents because of their tunable structures, large specific 
surface areas (SA), high porosities, and controllable functions [9]. Over 
the past decade, tens of thousands of MOFs have been synthesised and 
applied to gas adsorption and separation [10]. Studies on the adsorption 
of toxic pollutants such as antibiotics and heavy metals in water using 
MOFs have also been conducted but are in the early stages [1,11–13]. 
Theoretically, an infinite number of MOFs can be synthesised by 
assembling different metals and organic ligands. However, relying on 
extensive manual experiments to explore high-performance MOFs is a 
resource-intensive and inefficient trial-and-error process. Thus, a 
screening strategy to rapidly predict the adsorption performance of 
MOFs could have applications for screening superior adsorbents based 
on large databases of MOFs [14–16]. 
Data-driven machine learning (ML) is less dependent on prior 
knowledge than traditional programming methods and has the advan -
tages of a low computational cost and powerful predictive capability, 
demonstrating a promising future for solving complex environmental 
data issues [17]. In recent years, ML has shown significant potential for 
screening functional materials [18–23], predicting gas adsorption [24], 
exploring adsorption mechanisms, and simulating pollutant removal 
[25–30]. Nguyen et al. developed a ML model for predicting the equi -
librium adsorption capacity of biochar for drugs and explored the 
adsorption mechanism using literature data [25]. Molecular simulations 
combined with ML have become the new paradigm for discovering high- 
performance gas adsorbents through porous material screening. 
Recently, Wang et al. used an ML algorithm to explore the pattern of 
MOFs capturing chemical warfare agents from the air to assist in the 
screening of excellent MOFs [31]. However, the application of ML in 
adsorption is still in its infancy. In particular, a research gap remains in 
adsorbent screening for the removal of pollutants from water. It is 
difficult to obtain high-quality experimental data for ML modelling 
because of the scarcity of a direct database of sorption experiments that 
can be used for ML, which is the greatest obstacle to its development. 
In this study, an adsorbent screening strategy was proposed for the 
adsorptive removal of Cr(VI) from water. A purely data-driven ML 
approach was used to predict the adsorption performance using the 
characteristics of the MOFs and the experimental parameters of Cr(VI) 
adsorption as data sources. Six regression models were trained and 
evaluated, including random forest (RF) [32], gradient boosting deci -
sion tree (GBDT) [33], extreme gradient boosting tree (XGB) [34], 
support vector regression (SVR) [35], linear regression (LR), and K- 
nearest neighbour (KNN) [36,37]. The optimal model was used to 
simulate the adsorption of Cr(VI) by the MOFs based on valid experi -
mental parameters and the adsorption capacity was output. Potential 
high-performance adsorbents were screened using a combination of ML 
prediction and analysis. The feature importance and partial dependence 
plots (PDP) of the ML method were used to explain how the features 
affect the adsorption capacity and the degree of influence. The complete 
workflow for screening the MOF adsorbents is shown in Fig. 1. These 
results can accelerate the development of novel MOF adsorbents and 
serve as a reference for the rapid screening of high-performance MOFs 
for various operating scenarios in the future. 
2. Data and methods 
2.1. Data collection and pre-processing 
The data used in the ML process were mainly from peer-reviewed 
publications (2014–2021, Elsevier, RSC – Royal Society of Chemistry, 
ACS publications) and 801 data points covering Cr(VI) onto 43 MOFs 
were extracted. During the process of literature selection, we excluded 
studies with unknown sources or significant missing data. With the 
adsorption capacity (Q; mg/g) as the prediction target, the collected 
data were broadly classified into two categories: adsorbent properties, 
including coordination metal (MT), modification method (ModM), SA 
(m

experimental parameters, including initial concentration (CI; mg/L), 
adsorbent dose (AD; mg/L), reaction time (Time; min), pH, and reaction 
temperature (Tem; K). Because all data were manually collected from 
independent research literature without uniform standards and reli -
ability assessments, two assumptions were made during the process of 
establishing the dataset: (1) all experimental data obtained from the 
literature sources could be objectively accepted and (2) the impact of 
data errors caused by experimental processes and data collection in each 
study on ML outcomes could be neglected. All data points were collected 
directly from graphs or text in the literature. Representative samples 
were given priority and duplicate samples were retrieved and eliminated 
from the entire dataset. For samples with a high number of missing 
values, selective elimination was performed. The inconformity of aper -
ture data across publications may be detrimental to ML modelling. The 
theoretical mean PD (MPD; nm) was uniformly calculated and used as a 
feature. This approach can be understood as feature augmentation from 
a ML perspective. In addition, four quantitative variables, ionic charge 
(IC), atomic radius (AR), polarisability (Pol), and electronegativity (Ele), 
were used instead of the qualitative variable MT in the final dataset to 
benefit ML modelling. 
The dataset was organised into 801 rows and 12 columns. To miti -
gate the risk of data leakage during the ML process, the data were 
divided into 43 groups based on the MOFs and arranged in sequential 
order. The missing pH and Tem values were assumed to be 7 and 298 K, 
respectively. Partially missing values for the four variables were as fol -
lows: PD (16.2 %), PV (14.2 %), SA (6.7 %), and CI (0.3 %). The RF 
algorithm was used to interpolate the data and fill in the missing data. 
Dimensional differences in the quantitative variables may negatively 
affect the predictive performance of the model. To accommodate ML 
modelling, the quantitative variables were standardised before training. 
In addition, qualitative variables were labelled and encoded in advance.

The importance of each feature was calculated and ranked using the

method quantifies the impact of each feature on the forecasted results as 
a percentage and assesses the magnitude of the overall performance loss 
of the model after removing that feature [38] . 
2.3. Modelling and evaluation 
The dataset was divided into training and test sets and a prediction 
model was built using the RF algorithm. As shown in Fig. S4 , the pre -
diction performance of the model improves as the percentage of the 
training set increases from 20 % to 90 %. The learning ability of the 
model for the relationship between features and adsorption capacity was 
positively correlated with the predictive performance. Therefore, a 
training set that included 90 % of the data was built to train the model 
and tune its hyperparameters. A test set that included 10 % of the data 
was used to evaluate the model prediction performance. Different types 
of ML algorithms have fixed hyperparameters and tuning their impor -
tant hyperparameters to the optimum is the key to improve the model 
prediction performance. In the hyperparameters tuning process, the 
optimal hyperparameter combination was determined using the grid 
search method and 10-fold cross-validation after the parameter space of 
the model was determined [39,40] . The specific hyperparameter tuning 
process is described in the Text S1. In this way, an optimized prediction 
model is obtained. The test set was used to predict the adsorption ca -
pacity. The coefficient of determination ( R
2
) was calculated to evaluate 
the model performance and the mean absolute error (MAE) and root 
mean square error (RMSE) were applied to assess the model prediction 
accuracy. 
Six ML regression algorithms were utilised in this study to develop 
prediction models, including RF based on the bagging method, GBDT 
based on boosting, XGB, LR, KNN, and SVR with nonlinear kernel 
functions. After training, a comprehensive comparison of the perfor -
mance metrics among the six models, particularly R
2
, was conducted to 
select the high-performance models for simulating the adsorption ex -
periments. All the data processing and ML methods described here were 
performed in the Python programming language (version 3.8) using the 
open-source scikit-learn library [41] . 
2.4. Simulation of Cr(VI) adsorption by MOFs 
2.4.1. Determination of experimental parameters 
To facilitate the screening of adsorbents for Cr(VI) in water through 
simulation of adsorption by MOFs, it is necessary to determine effective 
experimental parameters in advance. A PDP was used to visualise how 
the experimental parameters affected the adsorption capacity. The PDP 
retained one or two target features as well as other marginalised fea -
tures. A sensitivity analysis of the target features was provided by PDP

2.4.3. Formulating simulation strategy 
Model fusion strategies have frequently been used to improve 
prediction accuracy because of the limitations of single models [44,45] . 
Moreover, owing to the limited data volume, the performance of the 
trained models may not be exceptionally robust. To further optimise the 
adsorbent screening, a multiple model combination strategy was used to 
simulate the adsorption experiments by connecting the first and second 
models in series for performance evaluation. First, under the determined 
experimental parameters, the best-performing model was employed to 
simulate the adsorption of Cr(VI) in water by the 3833 MOFs. The 
resulting adsorption capacity is referred to as the first adsorption data -
set, from which a subset of MOFs with higher adsorption capacities was 
selected. Subsequently, this subset of MOFs was used as the input for the 
second-best-performing model to further simulate the adsorption ex -
periments and the obtained adsorption capacity was designated as the 
second adsorption dataset.

2.5.3. Adsorbent fine screen 
However, in addition to possessing high adsorption capacity for Cr 
(VI) in water, they must also exhibit ideal stability as adsorbents. The 
Cambridge Crystallographic Data Centre (CCDC) provides comprehen -
sive information on MOFs, including constituent ingredients, structural 
information, and relevant publications which facilitates further analysis 
of material properties. 
For MOFs as adsorbent materials, high stability is essential to ensure 
the efficient removal of pollutants from water. Many researchers have 
proposed performance indicators for evaluating the stability of adsor -
bent materials, such as MOFs, including their stability after solvent 
removal, stability in aqueous solutions, and thermal stability [47,48] . 
The strength of the coordination bonds between the metal ions and 
organic ligands is a crucial factor affecting the stability of MOFs. Ac -
cording to the soft and hard acid-base theory, MOFs synthesised from 
hard acid metal ions and hard base ligands, as well as soft acid metal ions 
and soft base ligands, tend to exhibit higher stability in aqueous solu -
tions [49] . Nandy et al. collected data from the literature to create a 
dataset on the stability of MOFs for ML modelling. They tested the 
performance of the model and accurately predicted the post-solvent 
removal stability and the thermal stability of MOFs, showing an accu -
racy of 78 % and MAE of 55 
◦

3. Results and discussion 
3.1. Feature importance 
The contribution of each feature to adsorption capacity was deter -
mined using the RF algorithm. Fig. 2 (c) illustrates that the CI of Cr(VI) in 
the solution exhibited a significant influence, contributing 28 % of the 
overall contribution. This finding is consistent with those of a previous 
study [25] . This is because the heightened gradients of Cr(VI) could 
surpass the mass transfer resistance from the aqueous phase to the solid 
adsorbent, thus promoting both the adsorption kinetics of the pollutants 
on the adsorbent surface and the subsequent pore diffusion at the later 
stage [51] . The feature importance ranking of pH was fourth, making it 
the second-most influential experimental parameter after CI in terms of 
adsorption capacity. This can be attributed to the alteration of the MOFs ’ 
charge characteristics caused by varying the pH values, which conse -
quently affects the adsorption capacity for hexavalent chromium ions. In 
this study, it was crucial to establish effective experimental parameters 
before conducting the adsorption experiments. The ranking of impor -
tance of these parameters was as follows: CI > pH > AD > Time > Tem 
and the ranking of feature importance with respect to the adsorbent 
properties was MPD > ModM > PD > MT > PV > SA. MPD has the 
greatest influence on the adsorption capacity because the calculation of 
MPD is related to both the SA and PV of MOFs and, to some extent, 
encompasses the contribution of SA and PD. 
Notably, ModM had a remarkable effect on the adsorption capacity, 
with a contribution of 15 %. The modification of the pristine MOFs 
effectively boosted the adsorption of Cr(VI) in water. Recently, re -
searchers have introduced specific functional groups into MOFs to 
improve their adsorption of target pollutants. The dataset was divided 
into two parts: unmodified MOFs (4 1 6) and modified MOFs (3 8 5). The 
RF algorithm was employed to perform independent hyperparameter 
tuning on various datasets and train the models separately using the best 
hyperparameters to evaluate model performance. The results revealed 
that the MAE and RMSE of the former were 16.18 and 25.08, whereas 
those of the latter were 45.54 and 57.62, respectively. Regarding the 
modified MOFs, there were differences in the functional groups intro -
duced, modification methods, and parameters across different studies. 
Thus, the performance of the final trained model was influenced to some 
extent. Therefore, the final data used for screening did not include 
modification parameters and relied solely on the structural character -
istics of the MOF library to screen for superior MOF adsorbents.

adsorption processes was reached within 12 h. The impact of pH on the

3.5. Screening of adsorbents 
According to the established screening procedure, a hierarchical 
screening of 3833 MOFs was conducted based on the predicted 
adsorption capacity. This study prioritised the predicted data from the 
XGB model to select the top 10 MOFs (out of 70) for each class of co -
ordination metal MOFs from a total of 3833 MOFs. The 70 MOFs were 
then reranked based on the predicted data from the RF model to make 
further selections according to the classification of coordination metals. 
During the MOF selection process, preference was given to Zn- and Cu- 
based MOFs that were highly abundant in the dataset, as well as to Zr- 
based MOFs known for their superior stability. Ultimately, 20 MOFs 
stood out and are listed in Tables S3 and S4 . From the model itself, the 
screened 20 MOFs possess high Cr(VI) adsorption capacity in water. 
However, further analysis and screening are necessary to assess the 
feasibility of MOFs as adsorbents for the removal of pollutants from 
water. For this purpose, information regarding the composition, struc -
ture, and relevant literature for MOFs were collected and their stability 
and thermal stability after solvent removal were predicted. To establish 
a reference for further screening, the structure-adsorption capacity re -
lationships of the 20 MOFs were ranked. Because of the different coor -
dination metal types, the coordination metal information was 
normalised. The purpose of this normalization was to shield the poten -
tial influence of coordination metals on the adsorption performance. By 
setting the coordination metal feature to a uniform value, any variation 
in its effect on the predicted adsorption capacity among different MOFs 
was eliminated. This approach enables a more accurate comparison and 
evaluation of other structural features related to adsorption capacity 
among the MOFs. The XGB model was used to predict the adsorption 
capacity under the determined experimental parameters and was ranked 
as an indicator for further screening. Information regarding the 20 MOFs 
is presented in Table S3 .

4. Conclusions 
Using a data-driven ML method, this study identified XGB as the 
prediction model with the best model performance. A high-precision 
prediction of the Cr(VI) adsorption performance of the MOFs was ach -
ieved and the range of structural characteristics of the high-performance 
MOF adsorbents was determined. Although some published studies have 
presented views on predicting the performance of MOF adsorbents, 
challenges in screening persist [37,68 – 70] . This study proposed a MOF 
adsorbent screening strategy by mining the adsorption rules and 
revealing the structure – activity relationship, which was successfully 
implemented to screen seven MOFs with the best adsorption properties 
from 3833 MOFs and verified the adsorption properties of two MOF 
adsorbents with high potential, UiO-66 and MOF-801, through experi -
ments. This study provides new ideas for the optimisation and screening 
of adsorbents for the removal of Cr(VI) pollutants from water. 
Nevertheless, there is still room for improvement in the performance 
of models used to simulate adsorption experiments. In addition to 
insufficient data, another significant reason for this is the specific 
adsorption caused by the presence of specific functional groups in 
certain MOFs, which is also an influential factor in the adsorption ca -
pacity. These factors limit the ability of the data to fully support model 
learning. Further, in-depth studies are required to address these 
limitations.
